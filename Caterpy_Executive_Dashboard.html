What The Raw Data Proved

Six widely-held assumptions about this business were tested against the full dataset and statistically overturned. One assumption was confirmed. These reversals should change how the next 90 days are planned.

Executive Summary

Most "channel is broken" narratives in this business don't hold up statistically. The acquisition engine, the ROAS trend, and the checkout experience are all healthier than assumed. The real problems are a single cost-structure break in January 2025 and an email flow that was never activated — both fixable without new spend.

Checkout Abandonment
Overturned
Previous Assumption

"Checkout UX is broken and needs a redesign — abandonment is rising everywhere."

Actual Finding

Sessions/Order → Abandonment: r=0.77, p<0.001. Social & search abandonment are stable. Only direct traffic (returning customers) abandonment exploded 42%→66%.

Business Impact: This is an offer-quality problem for returning customers, not a site problem. A checkout redesign would waste budget.

January 2025 Shipping Break
Critical — New Finding

Shipping costs have been drifting up gradually with carrier rate inflation.

Actual Finding

Business Impact: A single contract/billing event, not inflation. One hour pulling carrier invoices could recover $500–$1,400/month.

A single cost-structure event in January 2025 has been quietly eroding margin on every order since — and it has never been investigated.

This is a confirmed structural break (not gradual drift) that has cost the business roughly $3,010/month in net shipping profit ever since. It has never been root-caused.

What Happened

Per-order outbound shipping cost was a flat $0.38 for 32 consecutive months (May 2022 – Dec 2024), then became a flat $5.12 from January 2025 onward — a 13.5× increase that took effect in a single month and has not moved since.

Evidence

Regression: Shipping Net = $1,912 + ($9.69 × month) − ($3,010 × post_Jan25). R²=0.780, F=81.8, p<0.0001, t(post_Jan25)=−7.84. The time-trend term alone is not significant (p=0.46) — ruling out gradual drift.

Financial Impact

Shipping net P&L: +$28,128 in 2023 → −$9,117 in 2025. Every order placed since January 2025 has carried roughly $4.74 more shipping cost than it did before, with no corresponding price change.

Why It Matters

This single line item is large enough to materially change the unit economics of every channel and promotion in this report. Any ROAS, AOV, or margin figure calculated after January 2025 already has this cost baked in — meaning the "true" picture, post-fix, is better than it currently appears.

Recommended Investigation Path

1) Pull Shopify Shipping carrier invoices for December 2024 and January 2025 side by side. 2) Check for a new carrier contract, rate-card update, or fulfillment provider change effective January 2025. 3) Check whether a "calculated shipping" integration was disabled and replaced with a flat-rate default. This is roughly one hour of work with a $500–1,400/month potential upside.

Key Decision

Treat this as the #1 priority investigation — above any marketing decision — because it affects every other number in this report.

Recommended Action

Assign someone to pull carrier invoices Dec 2024 vs Jan 2025 this week. This is a finance/ops task, not a marketing task.

Expected Business Impact

$500–1,400/month in recovered margin if it's a billing error — equivalent to 10–25% of current monthly net profit.

Plain-English Translation Of Every Regression Result

Statistical Findings

Every statistical test run on this dataset, translated into what it means for the business and what to do about it.

Executive Summary

Seven major statistical findings emerged from 49 months of data. Together they paint a consistent picture: the business's cost structure (not its marketing) drives profit, the acquisition engine is genuinely improving, spend changes take 3 months to show up, and traffic quality — not checkout design — drives abandonment.

Revenue vs Profit
High Confidence

Finding: Net Sales → Net Profit correlation is r=+0.24 (not significant). Full cost-driver model R²=0.23.

Business Meaning: More revenue does not reliably mean more profit in this business. Profit is governed by cost structure (shipping, COGS, fixed costs), which can move independently of sales volume.

Recommended Action: Track cost-per-order metrics (especially shipping) as a primary KPI alongside revenue — not as an afterthought.

Shipping Break Regression
Very High Confidence

Finding: Shipping Net = $1,912 + ($9.69 × month) − ($3,010 × post_Jan25). R²=0.780, p<0.0001. Time trend alone not significant (p=0.46).

Business Meaning: This was a sudden, single-event cost increase in January 2025 — not gradual inflation. Something changed in a contract, carrier, or billing setup at one specific point in time.

Recommended Action: Pull carrier invoices for Dec 2024 vs Jan 2025 — this is the #1 priority action in the entire report.

CPA Trend
High Confidence

Finding: Time → New Customer CPA: r=−0.46, p<0.001. CPA fell ~$0.15/month for 49 straight months. Spend level itself: not a significant CPA predictor (p=0.48).

Business Meaning: The acquisition system has gotten more efficient over time, independent of how much was spent. This is a genuinely positive long-term trend.

Recommended Action: Restart acquisition spend with confidence — CPA should land at or below the historical average (currently trending toward $12–15).

Spend Lag Effect
High Confidence

Finding: Spend(t−3) → Revenue(t): r=0.945 — the single strongest relationship in the dataset, peaking at a 3-month lag.

Business Meaning: Marketing spend decisions made today won't show their full revenue impact for roughly three months. Both cuts and increases follow this pattern.

Recommended Action: Set expectations for any spend restart with a 90-day evaluation window — don't judge results at 30 days.

Retention Lag Effect
Moderate Confidence

Finding: New customers acquired in month T correlate with returning customers in month T+12 at r=0.43 (p<0.01).

How To Read This Section

"r" measures the strength of a relationship (closer to 1 or −1 = stronger). "p<0.001" means there's less than a 0.1% chance the pattern is due to random noise — this is the threshold used for "very high confidence" findings. "R²" measures how much of the variation in one number is explained by another (0.78 = 78% explained).

Permanent Knowledge Base

What future teams must know

These ten findings are the most likely to be re-discovered the hard way if not documented. Each one overturned a prior assumption or surfaced a gap that had gone unnoticed for years. Treat this section as mandatory onboarding for any new hire, agency, or contractor touching this account.

Executive Summary

The single most expensive mistake a future team could make is re-litigating these findings from scratch. Each entry below documents what happened, why, the evidence behind it, and what to do differently. This is the institutional memory that left when prior teams left.

1. Net sales does not predict profit
What Happened

Profit stayed roughly flat ($1,340/mo avg in 2025) while revenue collapsed 84% from peak.

Why

Spend was cut roughly in proportion to revenue decline, preserving the cost/revenue ratio by coincidence, not design.

Evidence

Net Sales → Net Profit: r=+0.24, not significant. Full model R²=0.234.

What Future Teams Should Know

Do not use revenue trajectory as a proxy for business health. Track cost structure (shipping, COGS, app stack) as the primary profit driver.

2. CPA improves with time, not with spend level
What Happened

New customer CPA declined from ~$28 to ~$13 over 49 months — even as spend was cut dramatically.

Why

Audience and creative learnings compound over time independent of budget. Targeting got sharper; spend amount was not the driver.

Evidence

Time → New CPA: r=−0.46, p<0.001, β=−$0.15/month. Adspend level not a significant CPA predictor (p=0.48).

What Future Teams Should Know

Do not assume restarting spend will "reset" CPA upward. The efficiency gains are durable — but only volume restores revenue.

3. The acquisition engine was turned off, not broken
What Happened

New customers fell from 721/mo (2023 peak) to 92/mo (May 2026) — an 87% decline.

Why

The decline tracks almost exactly with the adspend reduction (r=+0.94 spend↔revenue). This is a volume story, not an efficiency story.

Evidence

Net Sales = $5,372 + ($1.28 × Adspend), R²=0.858. The $5,372 organic floor barely moved.

What Future Teams Should Know

The channel works. Restarting spend at proven-efficient levels (Google, Nicole+BOGO) should restore volume at or below historical CPA.

4. Spend decisions take 3 months to fully show up in revenue
What Happened

Revenue declines and recoveries consistently lag spend changes by approximately 3 months.

Why

Acquisition → first purchase → repeat purchase cycle plus campaign learning periods create natural lag.

Evidence

Spend(t−3) → Revenue(t): r=0.945, the strongest correlation in the entire dataset.

What Future Teams Should Know

Do not judge a spend change after 30 days. Evaluate at 90 days minimum. Cuts made now won't show full damage until Q3–Q4.

5. Checkout abandonment is a returning-customer problem, not a UX problem
What Happened

Checkout abandonment rose from 38% to 52% — but only for direct/returning traffic. Social and search abandonment barely moved.

Why

Sessions-per-order ratio explains 60% of abandonment variance. Returning customers see the $5.12 shipping cost and a $30 threshold that requires a second pair.

Evidence

Sessions/Order → Abandon%: r=+0.772, p<0.001, β=+0.95pp per unit.

What Future Teams Should Know

Do not commission a checkout redesign to fix this. Fix the shipping threshold and cost-visibility first — that is the actual lever.

6. BOGO is the structural offer for this business
What Happened

Every channel — Meta, email, discount codes — shows BOGO outperforming alternative promotions.

Why

The product (laces) has a natural pairing logic (one set of laces per shoe = need for two), making BOGO inherently aligned with usage.

Evidence

BOGO: 2.07× ROAS vs 1.27× no-promo on Meta. Nicole+BOGO creative variants: 3.0–3.44× ROAS.

What Future Teams Should Know

Default to BOGO as the baseline offer. Test alternatives against BOGO as the benchmark, not against "no offer."

7. The January 2025 shipping break was never investigated
What Happened

Outbound shipping cost per order jumped from $0.38 to $5.12 — a 13.5× increase — in a single month and has held steady since.

Why

Unknown. No documentation exists of a carrier contract change, rate negotiation, or fulfillment provider switch around this date.

Evidence

Regression confirms an abrupt structural break (β=−$3,010/mo, p<0.0001), not gradual drift (time trend p=0.46, not significant).

What Future Teams Should Know

This is the single highest-value unanswered question in the business. One hour reviewing Dec 2024 vs Jan 2025 carrier invoices could recover $500–1,400/month.

8. Email volume does not drive email revenue — flows do
What Happened

144 B2C campaigns generated $102K lifetime. One automated flow (Welcome) generated $153K.

Why

Flows are triggered at high-intent moments (signup, cart abandon). Campaigns are broadcast and compete with inbox fatigue.

Evidence

Emails Sent → Net Sales: r=+0.012, not significant. Welcome flow RPR: $5.35 vs campaign average RPR far lower.

What Future Teams Should Know

Prioritize flow coverage (especially the dormant checkout flow) over campaign send frequency. More campaigns ≠ more revenue.

9. BWP (Amazon) is the most underanalyzed asset in the business
What Happened

BWP revenue grew 213% from 2023 to 2025 with zero marketing spend attributed to it — and nobody has audited its unit economics.

Why

It was treated as a passive listing rather than an active channel, so it never received analytical attention proportional to its growth.

Evidence

BWP shows 3.0× attributed ROAS on a $0 CAC basis — but Amazon fees, returns, and fulfillment costs are not yet modeled.

What Future Teams Should Know

This channel could be either a hidden profit center or a margin trap depending on true fee structure. Audit before any further investment.

10. The 2025 retention deficit is already locked into 2026–27
What Happened

New customer acquisition collapsed in 2025. Those missing customers were also missing future returning-customer revenue.

Why

New customers acquired in month T statistically predict returning customers in month T+12 — a 12-month retention pipeline.

Evidence

New customers(t) → Returning(t+12): r=0.430, p<0.01.

What Future Teams Should Know

Even if acquisition is fixed today, 2026–27 returning-customer revenue will still reflect the 2025 shortfall. This is a multi-year recovery, not a quarterly fix — set expectations accordingly.
